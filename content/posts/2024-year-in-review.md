This has undoubtably been one of the most chaotic years of my life so far.
Looking back to how it all started, I remember sitting with a close friend as
the clock struck midnight on New Year's Eve and discussing what our aims for
the next 366 days were.

The autumn of the previous year had seen a breakup and reconnection with my
partner at the time. I was adamant that I wanted to focus my energy on
repairing that relationship, sacrificing whatever was required to bring us back
to a happy and healthy state. I'd been in relationships of some form for almost
8 years by that point and I think I defined a lot of my identity by it.

## Going Downhill

Two weeks later, we separated again with at least some mutual understanding
that it wasn't working out. I think I saw this coming in the prior days, but
even then it's never a pleasant experience. The first week felt alright, the
anxiety and worry cleared up from noticing what was happening.

From there it got a lot tougher. Breakups are difficult for both parties to
process, but especially on the receiving end. There's a whole wealth of hopes
built up for the future and it can feel like those have collapsed. Dates that
you once looked forward to suddenly become harsh reminders of the other person
and what could have been, if events had gone the way you wanted.

Time begins to move slowly, your feelings swamped with regret and self-blame,
your thoughts filled with ways you think you could have saved it. This was
certainly true for me, I remember joking with a colleague about how much more I
could have achieved at work if I didn't spend so much time mulling over these
ideas.

## Understanding Myself

There's two main paths that most people take after a breakup. The first is to
immediately try and get into another relationship, known as a rebound.
Sometimes this goes well, but I think a lot of the time it's out of a desire to
replace the person you've lost and not predominantly because you're interested
in the new one.

The other approach is to take time to yourself, perhaps force yourself to be
alone for a while. I appreciated that I'd been in relationships almost non-stop
since the age of 16, almost 8 years by the start of the year. Even if I felt
like I had lost something and needed to replace it, I knew that it was time to
be on my own for a while and begin to understand what that felt like.

As such, this year has been a lot about experimentation for me. I've tried to
explore new things and make myself uncomfortable, understanding whether that
was just because it was new or because it didn't feel like me. While the first
half of the year had a few of these points (doing a half marathon, trying to
integrate with new groups of friends), the second half is where this really
took off.

## Summer Hecticness

One thing that's always made me nervous and uncomfortable was travel. Growing
up, my parents broadly handled everything for me and we'd always go to the same
familiar places. 2023 marked the first time where I travelled on my own as well
as a small solo trip to the Netherlands, but I felt like I needed to go further
than that. What always filled me with anxiety was moving around and not having
everything at my disposal, so we needed to tackle that.

The culmination of this was a two and a half week long solo trip across Europe,
largely interrailing around. Every country I visited was totally new to me,
with different languages and cultures. I stayed in hostels for the first time,
changing where I was staying every other day and only booking my next one when
I arrived at the previous. While I had a reasonable itinerary in terms of the
route, it changed as I met people and exchanged recommendations. At the end of
it all I'd travelled to 13 different places across Belgium, Germany, Austria,
Slovakia and Hungary.

Overall, this can probably be described as a formative experience. Plenty of
days felt uncomfortable and lonely, but equally many had me being more
spontaneous and getting to know new people in places I was unfamiliar with. It
proved to me that I didn't need to worry so much about travel, and even on a
trip with such little planning it would all work itself out.

The weeks after my return were equally hectic. Just a week after my return I
was flying out to Dublin to see my sister with my parents, for a 2 week trip
around Ireland and Northern Ireland. We spent a few days in Dublin itself
before heading down to a beautiful countryside home in Cork, where I decided to
run a half marathon around the hilly roads. We then made our way back up to
Belfast for my sister's graduation.

One week later I stepped out of my comfort zone again, with a trip to the Gower
Peninsula near Swansea for the Love Trails Festival. This was only a few days
long, but brought me back to my time in the Scouts as we camped in a field by
night and ran around the countryside by day. Absolutely exhausting, but another
experience that felt unfamiliar.

Another week passed by and I headed to the Netherlands for the Liquicity
Festival (my second time). By this point I was pretty comfortable staying in
hostels, and met some people from New Zealand and Belgium who were taking the
festival seriously to say the least. Having spent the last 2 months travelling
around I was pretty shattered, but still had a great time as always.

## Becoming a Classic 25 Year Old

My interrailing trip included my 25th birthday, and as everyone in London knows
this requires you to run a marathon. Through the running club I was part of I'd
been at the London marathon that year, and people were discussing the
possibility of entering the Amsterdam marathon in October. I'd just run a half
marathon a few weeks prior and struggled with the experience, but reckoned that
I could get it together for a full. Granted, there's no time like the present.

Someone acquired a ticket for me at the start of August, which meant it was
time to get training. I started averaging more than 40km of running a week,
alongside all the other training I was doing. Looking back on this I wish I'd
done a proper plan, but I was just pushing my body a bit further each week and
trying to get it used to the longer runs. In the end this proved to be my
downfall, I blistered very badly a few weeks out and had to take a couple weeks
off running.

The marathon itself was a great experience. There was so much tension and
nervousness at the start that my heart rate immediately spiked to 180 beats per
minute, which wasn't sustainable. I couldn't get it back down after that, so I
was pretty shot by the halfway point. From there it was a constant fluctuation
between walking and running as my hips slowly ceased up alongside some doubt
that I'd ever finish, but I crossed the finish line after 4 hours and 19
minutes of pain.

Looking back on the experience, I told myself when I finished the half marathon
in April that I'd never do another one again, but the marathon didn't inspire
the same view. I knew I wasn't desperate to do another, but I felt good about
the journey and the outcome. It wasn't as fast as I'd hoped, but that left
opportunity for growth.

## Winter Renaissance

With all the travelling I'd done in June and July alongside the desire to train
consistently for the marathon, by the end of October I still had plenty of
annual leave to use up. I knew this would have to be another solo trip on the
basis that it was getting to the end of the year and I'd left little time for
planning, but that actually turned out well.

15 days of annual leave translated to 3 weeks of time off, so I had plenty of
options. I did consider going further afield than Europe such as South America
or Asia, but these all felt like places I'd like to experience for the first
time with someone else.

I'd mentally planned out a trip to the Baltics (Finland, Estonia, Latvia and
Lithuania) as they were all neighbours, but ended up getting in touch with a
friend from university who was doing a Masters degree in Geneva, Switzerland at
the time. From there I planned backwards.

We agreed to meet in Lausanne, so I accounted for some time in Switzerland. I'd
seen that I could get a night train from Berlin to Zurich which would tick a
lot of boxes, I'd never been on one before so it would be a new experience, it
was convenient for the trip I was doing and I didn't get to visit Berlin during
the summer. There were good flights to Helsinki and I could easily get the
ferry over to Tallinn afterwards, so I just needed a route between there and
Germany.

The intention was to get the bus through Latvia and Lithuania, but this proved
to take too long considering I was only passing through the countries. In the
end I flew from Tallinn to Berlin, completing my ring-route after I stopped in
Paris on the way back for a bagel.

Looking back on this trip and comparing it to the summer variant, it's easy to
see progress in myself. I was so much more comfortable in my own skin and being
alone in uncomfortable situations, happy to afford myself the time to relax and
unwind instead of trying to do everything. No doubt there were lonely segments
as well, but the overall experience felt more in touch with myself.

## Quarterlife

Midlife crises are a well researched and documented phenomenon of self
reflection in indivduals between the ages of 45 and 65. It's a stage where
people begin to question their life choices, morality and identity.

Throughout this year, I've frequently described myself as having a quarterlife
crisis. So many of the same questions went through my head, about who I was and
what I wanted to be, but brought on earlier by what transpired at the beginning
of the year and conversations I'd had prior to that.

I'd taken a book called Atomic Habits with me on the trip, but finished it by
the time I arrived in Tallinn. Stumbling upon a bookstore in the middle of
town, I figured I might as well see if they had any books in English. There was
a small section at the back containing these, and one called Quarterlife by
Satya Doyle Byock caught my eye.

It details the stories of 4 fictional characters going through their own
quarterlife crises. The book separates them into 2 different types, denoted as
Meaning and Stability. This part resonated well with me, I'd spent the last 10
years of my life unknowingly focusing on the stability part and was now coming
to terms with the fact I hadn't developed the meaning portion in the same way.

Much like one of the characters in the book, my life was great in theory. I had
a well paying job that I loved working at, both in terms of the people and the
day-to-day duties, a close group of friends and family. I was in shape and
constantly active, spurred on by all the running and a desire to be able to
move a little more comfortably after my powerlifting days.

Unfortunately, there still felt like a looming problem that I didn't have the
answer to. My stability was largely under control but I lacked the
understanding of who I was and what it all meant.

In a lot of ways, the book did less in the way of answering these questions for
me but reassured me that it was both normal to feel like this and that I was
going about the situation in the right way. It talked about experimentation,
about trying new things and learning what you like or dislike, about forcing
yourself outside of your comfort zone. I drew parallels between the characters
and myself, finding some solitude that the portrayals sometimes felt like me.

## Running Clubs

In mid-February, a colleague invited me to a run club. Oddly enough, they
weren't actually there (it was another mutual colleague we both worked at who
went regularly) but I'd say this was another defining point of the year for me
even if I didn't know it at the time.

As is the theme for a lot of the content of this review, it was a step outside
my comfort zone. While I had a good group of friends from university, I hadn't
really made many more since leaving Warwick. The running club introduced me to
new people so quickly I couldn't remember their names and each week I had the
opportunity to force myself to interact with someone new. I'd definitely been
left with some anxiety after the events at the start of the year and this
constant requirement to start conversations quelled some of it.

Even though I joined back in February, proper friendships take time and work to
build. Towards the end of the year I joined onto the trip heading to Málaga for
the marathon which turned out to be a great idea and definitely a highlight of
the year.

Even if we didn't end up doing too much exploration of the city itself, it was
spontaneous and out of my comfort zone, and allowed me to return to the
feelings I used to love so much from handling athletes at powerlifting
competitions. It's a great experience to take as much of someone else's plate
and allow them to perform at the highest level they can.

There were other standout points of the running club, such as:

* Supporting at the London Marathon and Hackney Half
* Hanging out with people after Amsterdam Marathon
* The hecticness of Love Trails Festival

Málaga still rests above those though, for the bonding it afforded me with the
others there as well as the breath of fresh air after travelling solo for the
whole year.
